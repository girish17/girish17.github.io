---
title: Software Philosophy – Free or Not Free?
category: FLOSS
excerpt: Exploring the spectrum between free (as in freedom) and proprietary software, and the challenges of leading a life focused on software freedom in a digital world.
---

I find myself somewhere between the extremes of software philosophy: free (as in freedom) software on one end and proprietary software on the other. More than a year ago, I chose to eliminate proprietary software from my personal and professional life. Influenced by Richard Stallman (RMS), the pioneer of the free software movement, I consciously decided to give up non-free software. This decision led me to forgo job opportunities that relied extensively on proprietary tools. Instead, I began creating free software independently, building integrations that are completely open for copying, modification, and redistribution. As an author, I ensure my programs are available under the GNU General Public License v3.0 or later, which guarantees copyleft—a legally binding rule ensuring my programs can never be made proprietary by anyone.

How does software freedom help? The most compelling reason is that code published under a free license can be audited freely by anyone. This transparency allows people to suggest changes, fix bugs, and collaborate openly to build secure, ethical, and freedom-respecting software. The core of free software is the liberty to develop, modify, and distribute programs. It boils down to a simple question: does the program control the user, or does the user control the program? The latter is the only natural expectation. Users should have the right to inspect, tweak, and redistribute software without fear of patent infringement or copyright violation.

Despite my constant efforts, I still encounter hurdles. I am often forced to use proprietary messaging tools like WhatsApp due to the network effect of family and friends. Banks, government institutions, and service providers also frequently require proprietary software for transactions and essential services.

The pressure from businesses and online shops to use digital payment methods and e-wallets is another threat to my freedom, as these systems rely on proprietary gateways. Leading a reasonable life in modern society often compels me to use proprietary software at some stage. The lack of broad awareness puts advocates of software freedom at a disadvantage. I still believe that cash is the freest form of payment. Yet, more businesses are denouncing cash in favor of digital modes that link bank accounts to proprietary payment gateways. Without access to the source code, I cannot fully trust these systems with my private information, as they pose significant risks to data privacy and security.

How can anyone trust a payment gateway without knowing how it works? For the general public, there is no way to verify whether proprietary software is benevolent or malicious. Instead, we are expected to blindly trust that these systems are ethical—which is more often than not, false. These providers act from a position of immense power over users' information and personal lives. No one wants to give up such control unless there is a collective move toward software freedom. If people choose to move away from non-free software, it will spark a revolution for the betterment of society.
