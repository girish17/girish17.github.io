---
title: My Free Software Journey
category: FLOSS
excerpt: A personal account of transitioning to free software, the challenges of software freedom, and using 100% free software for business.
---

My motivation to write and contribute to free software has always been driven by both freedom and financial sustainability. For me, writing—both code and text—has been a primary means of income and survival for the last few years.

I previously worked for large companies that paid handsomely for developing proprietary software. The transition to free software was challenging; what led me to it was a larger cause: the freedom of users, including myself. I still struggle to wrap my head around the notion of 'freedom' as an abstract concept and its specific applicability to software. Is software freedom truly that important when other fundamental freedoms are constantly at stake? Freedom of speech, expression, movement, and identity—all of these feel more immediate than software freedom. While software freedom can support these rights, it cannot exist as an isolated moral principle. Human freedom must always take center stage. 

Furthermore, the notion that 'proprietary software is fundamentally unethical' is a flawed argument. While there are certainly examples of unethical designs and implementations, that label shouldn't apply to all proprietary software. It is not a zero-sum proposition. That said, free software is generally more benevolent and freedom-respecting, though it may not yet satisfy every user requirement.

Free software has helped me learn about development, implementation, design, version control, collaboration, and community building. This has been an exciting journey that has spanned over a decade and continues to evolve. Over the last few years, I have met many contributors and learned a great deal from them, particularly regarding the challenges of earning a living through free software. One such example is SavaPage, a Free/Libre Open Source (FLOSS) printing portal founded by Rijk Ravestein in the Netherlands. The project was recognized by the FSF as free software over a year ago. Even though SavaPage has users worldwide—across Africa, Asia, and Europe—generating sufficient revenue for business expansion remains a hurdle. I learned about free software advocacy and business practices from Rijk, a dedicated practitioner who helps others adopt these models.

My journey of using free software for business began with a teaching gig for students in the US who wanted to learn Python. I used Jitsi, a free video communication tool, for our sessions, and Xournal for whiteboard work and notes. My entire setup ran on Trisquel GNU/Linux on a Libreboot X200s machine—a 100% free software environment with no proprietary blobs, not even in the firmware. I was paid for the gig, and the students were happy to learn about computer fundamentals and software freedom alongside programming, a contrast to the proprietary-focused education they received in school. I continue to teach these students and am actively seeking others who want to learn the fundamentals of programming, mathematics, and free software. My services are listed in the FSF directory for this purpose. Additionally, I developed a free software integration for OpenProject (a fork of Redmine) for the Mattermost team collaboration platform. This integration relies on donations to support its infrastructure and new features, despite recent sponsorship from the OpenProject Foundation in Berlin.
